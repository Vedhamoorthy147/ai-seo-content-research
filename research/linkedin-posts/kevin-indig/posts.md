Kevin Indig – Key Insights from Recent LinkedIn Posts

1. SEO and AEO Are Related, But Not the Same

A common mistake people make is treating Answer Engine Optimization (AEO) as simply another name for SEO. While the two share many fundamentals, AEO introduces a different set of challenges.

Both SEO and AEO still depend on strong technical foundations. Content needs to be accessible to machines, websites need clear information architecture, and high-quality content remains essential. Authority signals such as expert contributions, citations, links, and brand mentions also continue to matter. Most importantly, content must align with what users are actually trying to accomplish.

Where AEO differs is in how AI systems interpret and present information. Instead of focusing solely on rankings, AI-generated answers evaluate brands based on how they are discussed across the web. Mentions in reviews, forums, comparison articles, documentation, and third-party content can influence visibility as much as traditional backlinks.

Another challenge is that prompts are far more complex than keywords. AI queries tend to be longer and more nuanced, making them difficult to target with individual pages. At the same time, AI systems build their own understanding of a company's category, competitors, strengths, and use cases from information gathered across many sources.

As a result, a brand's overall reputation becomes compressed into a single answer. Owned content, customer reviews, media coverage, and public sentiment are all combined into the way AI systems describe a business.

2. High-Reasoning Models Search Differently

Kevin shared research comparing how GPT-5.2 responds when using minimal reasoning versus high reasoning across different stages of the buyer journey.

One of the most interesting findings was that the difference in citation behavior is much larger at the beginning of the buying process. For problem-identification queries, high-reasoning responses cited sources significantly more often than minimal-reasoning responses. This suggests that advanced reasoning modes treat early-stage questions as research tasks and actively retrieve information rather than relying on existing knowledge.

The study also showed that high-reasoning models perform many more supporting searches, particularly during comparison and selection stages. Instead of answering a prompt with a single retrieval, the model may break the task into dozens of smaller searches.

For example, a CRM comparison prompt might trigger separate searches for API limits, pricing structures, compliance certifications, authentication methods, integrations, and other technical details. Each subtopic becomes its own retrieval task.

The implication for marketers is important: visibility is no longer just about ranking for a broad category keyword. Brands also need documentation and content that can be discovered for the many smaller questions AI systems ask behind the scenes.

3. AI Search Results Are More Fragmented Than Expected

Another insight from Kevin's research challenges the assumption that different AI search engines tend to cite the same sources.

Analyzing millions of URLs across commercial and informational queries, he found that overlap between cited sources remains surprisingly low. Even when prompts have clear commercial intent—such as searching for the best CRM software or project management tool—different AI systems frequently surface different sources.

This suggests that the retrieval systems behind each model play a bigger role than user intent. What an AI engine trusts, how it evaluates authority, and which content formats it prefers can vary significantly from one platform to another.

For businesses investing in AI visibility, this creates a challenge. Success in one platform does not automatically translate to success elsewhere. A guide that performs well in Perplexity may have little visibility in ChatGPT or Google AI Overviews.

The broader lesson is that AI search visibility is fragmented. Optimizing for a single AI platform should not be treated as a universal strategy. Brands need broader coverage across multiple ecosystems if they want consistent visibility in AI-generated answers.
